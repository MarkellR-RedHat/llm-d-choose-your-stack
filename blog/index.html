1. Introduction: The Problem with "Just Deploy It"

I've watched the same movie play out at dozens of organizations. The proof-of-concept works. A single vLLM instance on a rented GPU handles the demo traffic. Leadership is impressed. Then come the questions that unravel everything: What happens when traffic doubles next Tuesday? How do we give the fraud team their own fine-tuned adapter without doubling GPU spend? Why does time to first token spike when the support team's shift starts? Why is our GPU utilization at 30% even though users are complaining about latency?

The gap between "vLLM can serve this model" and "we have a production inference platform" is enormous. And that gap is where teams lose months. Not because the technology is hard, but because decision paralysis is hard. There are too many components, too many blog posts comparing frameworks, too many "it depends" answers that don't actually help you ship.

So let me be direct: vLLM is an inference engine, and it is an excellent one. But an engine is not a car. You still need routing that understands where your KV cache lives. You need autoscaling that responds to inference-specific metrics, not generic CPU utilization. You need monitoring that tracks time to first token and tokens per second, not just HTTP 200 rates. You need a multi-tenancy story that does not involve running separate clusters for every team that wants to serve a LoRA adapter.

This is the problem that llm-d solves. It is a CNCF Sandbox project that assembles the complete inference platform from Kubernetes-native components: vLLM for inference, the Endpoint Picker Plugin (EPP) for cache-aware routing, Gateway API for traffic management, KServe for model lifecycle, and Prometheus for observability. Each component is optional. You adopt what you need and skip what you do not.

But "which components do you need?" is exactly the question that trips teams up. I'm going to kill that question in the next 28 minutes. The answer depends on five dimensions: your model size, your latency targets, your traffic shape, your GPU budget, and your deployment environment. Those five dimensions produce a surprisingly small number of practical architectures. This post maps the decision space. The companion Choose Your Own Inference Stack interactive tool lets you explore it dynamically.

2. The Decision Framework: Five Dimensions That Define Your Stack

The most common mistake in inference architecture is treating component selection as a shopping list. Teams pick components based on feature comparison charts and end up with a stack that is technically capable of everything but optimized for nothing. Stop doing that. Start with your constraints and work backward to components.

There are five dimensions that matter:

Model size is the load-bearing wall. A 7B parameter model on a single GPU is a fundamentally different operational problem than a 70B model sharded across four GPUs. Model size determines your GPU topology, your minimum deployment unit, your scaling granularity, and your cost floor. Everything else is downstream of this choice.

Latency SLO determines your architecture's shape. If your p95 time to first token target is 2 seconds, you can use a unified serving pool and batch aggressively. If it is 200 milliseconds, you likely need prefill/decode disaggregation and prefix caching. The difference between these two architectures is not incremental; it is structural.

Traffic pattern determines your routing strategy. Steady batch traffic wants maximum throughput. Bursty interactive traffic wants responsive autoscaling. Agentic multi-turn traffic wants prefix-cache-aware routing that keeps sessions sticky to the pod holding their KV cache. A routing layer that is unaware of these patterns leaves 30-60% of your potential performance on the table.

GPU budget is the constraint that makes everything else real. There is always a theoretically better architecture that uses more GPUs. The practical question is: given N GPUs, what architecture extracts the most value? This dimension also determines how aggressively you can pursue redundancy, whether you can afford dedicated prefill and decode pools, and whether you need to share base model replicas across multiple tenants via LoRA adapters.

Deployment environment determines your operational ceiling. An on-premise Kubernetes cluster with a three-person SRE team has different constraints than a managed cloud environment with auto-provisioned node pools. The right architecture is one that your team can actually operate, monitor, and debug at 2 AM when something breaks.

💡 Key insight These dimensions are not independent sliders. Model size constrains GPU choice. Traffic pattern determines whether cache-aware routing delivers value. Latency targets determine whether disaggregation is worth the operational complexity. The danger is optimizing one dimension while ignoring its interactions with the others. A 70B model with aggressive latency targets and a small GPU budget is an unsolvable equation; something has to give.

3. Component Deep Dives: What You Need and When

llm-d is a composable platform, not a monolith. Each component addresses a specific operational need. Here is when you need each one and when you do not.

vLLM Engine

When you need it: Always. This is the inference runtime. vLLM handles model loading, continuous batching, PagedAttention for KV cache management, tensor parallelism for multi-GPU serving, and quantized model execution. There is no llm-d deployment without vLLM.

The key configuration decisions are tensor parallelism degree (how many GPUs per replica), prefix caching (enabled by default in recent versions, critical for multi-turn workloads), and quantization format (FP16, FP8, AWQ, GPTQ). These choices are largely determined by your model size and GPU hardware, and they are set at deployment time in your model serving configuration.

Endpoint Picker Plugin (EPP)

When you need it: As soon as you have more than one replica. The EPP is the routing brain. It maintains a real-time view of each vLLM replica's state: queue depth, KV cache contents, loaded LoRA adapters, and available capacity. When a request arrives, the EPP scores each replica based on cache hit potential, current load, and adapter availability, then routes to the optimal target.

When you can skip it: If you are running a single replica, there is no routing decision to make. But the moment you scale to two or more replicas, round-robin or random routing wastes prefill computation. The EPP is typically the single highest-impact component you can add after the base vLLM engine.

Gateway API

When you need it: When external traffic reaches your inference stack. The Gateway API provides standard Kubernetes ingress with inference-aware extensions: rate limiting per model, request prioritization, traffic splitting for canary deployments, and TLS termination. It integrates with the EPP so that routing decisions are made with full awareness of model-serving state.

When you can skip it: Internal-only workloads where services call the inference endpoint directly via ClusterIP can defer this. But anything exposed to end users or partner APIs needs a proper gateway.

KServe / InferencePool

When you need it: For managed model lifecycle. KServe handles model loading from object storage, health checking, readiness probing, rolling updates, and canary rollouts. InferencePool defines a logical group of model-serving pods that the EPP treats as a routing target. Together, they provide the operational layer that turns "a container running vLLM" into "a managed model endpoint."

Prometheus + Grafana

When you need it: In production. Always. vLLM exports a rich set of metrics: time to first token (TTFT), inter-token latency, tokens per second (TPS), queue depth, KV cache utilization, prefix cache hit rate, GPU memory usage, and batch size distribution. Without these metrics, you are operating blind. You cannot tune what you cannot measure, and inference workloads have enough moving parts that intuition alone will mislead you.

📈 Metrics that matter most Start with four dashboards: (1) TTFT by percentile, which tells you if users are waiting too long for the first token; (2) KV cache hit rate, which tells you if your routing is working; (3) GPU utilization versus queue depth, which tells you if you are compute-bound or scheduling-bound; and (4) tokens per second per replica, which tells you if any replica is degraded.

HPA Autoscaler

When you need it: For variable traffic. The Horizontal Pod Autoscaler scales your replica count based on custom metrics from vLLM, typically queue depth or pending request count. Scaling on CPU utilization is meaningless for GPU inference. You need to scale on inference-specific signals: when requests are queuing, add capacity; when GPUs are idle, release them.

When you can skip it: Steady batch workloads with predictable traffic can use a fixed replica count. But any user-facing workload with variable traffic patterns needs autoscaling to avoid either wasting GPU hours or dropping requests.

Prefill/Decode Disaggregation

When you need it: When you have aggressive latency targets on long-context inputs. Prefill (processing the input prompt) is compute-bound. Decode (generating output tokens) is memory-bandwidth-bound. In a unified pool, long prefill operations block decode operations, causing latency spikes for all concurrent requests. Disaggregation separates these phases onto dedicated GPU pools so that neither blocks the other.

When you can skip it: If your prompts are short (under 1,000 tokens), your models are small (under 13B parameters), or your latency targets are relaxed (TTFT above 2 seconds), the complexity of disaggregation is not justified. It roughly doubles your operational surface area since you are now managing two GPU pools, a KV cache transfer mechanism, and cross-pool routing logic.

4. Stack Patterns by Use Case

Abstract frameworks are useful, but concrete patterns are what teams actually deploy. Here are five production patterns, each with specific architecture choices and the reasoning behind them.

Chatbot / Customer Support

Multi-turn conversation is the most common production use case, and it is the one where cache-aware routing pays the biggest dividends. Each turn in a conversation appends a few hundred tokens to a growing context window. Without prefix caching, every turn re-processes the entire conversation history from scratch. With it, only the new tokens require prefill computation.

Architecture: vLLM with prefix caching enabled, EPP with session-affinity scoring, Gateway API for external traffic, HPA scaling on queue depth. Typical models are in the 7B to 70B range, often fine-tuned or LoRA-adapted for the support domain.

Expected performance: With prefix caching and cache-aware routing, TTFT on subsequent turns drops by 60-85% compared to naive round-robin routing. For a 5-turn conversation on a 13B model, this means the difference between 800ms TTFT on turn five (without cache routing) and 120ms (with it).

Code Completion / Copilot

Code completion is latency-critical in a way that chatbots are not. A developer typing code expects suggestions within 100-200ms. Anything slower feels broken. The prompts are typically short (a few hundred tokens of surrounding code context), but the request rate is extremely high since every keystroke or pause can trigger a completion request.

Architecture: Small, fast model (7B-13B), vLLM with speculative decoding if supported, EPP for load-aware routing (prefix caching matters less with short prompts), aggressive HPA scaling with fast pod startup. FP8 quantization to maximize throughput per GPU.

Key config: Set max_num_seqs high to handle the concurrency. Use gpu_memory_utilization at 0.90+ to maximize KV cache space for concurrent requests. Tune max_model_len down if you know your prompts are short; this frees memory for more concurrent sequences.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)

RAG workloads have a distinctive traffic pattern: variable-length prompts constructed at runtime from retrieved documents, with significant prefix overlap when multiple users query the same knowledge base. The system prompt, retrieval instructions, and frequently-retrieved documents create shared prefixes across requests.

Architecture: vLLM with prefix caching, EPP with prefix-aware routing, models in the 13B-70B range depending on reasoning requirements. Long context support is critical, as retrieved documents can push prompts to 8K-32K tokens.

⚡ RAG prefix caching pattern In a well-designed RAG system, the system prompt and retrieval template form a common prefix shared by every request. If you route requests to the same replica, that prefix is computed once and cached. For a 2,000-token system prompt, this saves 30-50ms of prefill per request at the 13B scale, a meaningful saving at thousands of requests per minute.

Batch Processing

Batch workloads (document summarization, data extraction, classification pipelines) optimize for throughput and cost, not latency. The goal is maximum tokens per dollar per hour.

Architecture: vLLM with large batch sizes, load-aware routing (cache routing is less relevant since prompts are typically unique), no autoscaling (fixed replica count sized for the job), aggressive quantization (FP8 or INT4) to maximize throughput. Consider pipeline parallelism for very large models.

Key config: Set max_num_seqs to the maximum your GPU memory supports. Disable or deprioritize TTFT optimization in favor of aggregate throughput. Use offline batching with the vLLM batch API rather than the OpenAI-compatible streaming endpoint.

Real-time Classification / Extraction

Structured extraction and classification tasks use small models with short prompts and require extreme low latency. Think: extracting entities from a customer message before routing it, classifying transaction risk in a payment pipeline, or parsing form fields from scanned documents.

Architecture: Small model (1B-7B), single GPU per replica, FP8 quantization, EPP for load balancing across many replicas, aggressive HPA scaling. These workloads are closer to traditional microservice traffic patterns: high concurrency, short request duration, low tolerance for tail latency.

Expected performance: A 3B model on a single H100 with FP8 can process classification requests with sub-50ms TTFT and generate 5-10 output tokens in under 20ms. At this scale, the bottleneck shifts from GPU compute to network overhead and routing latency.

5. The Complexity-Performance Tradeoff

Every component in your inference stack adds operational surface area. Each one introduces new failure modes, new configuration knobs, new metrics to monitor, and new on-call runbooks to write. The question is not "does this component improve performance?" (it almost always does) but "does the improvement justify the operational cost for my specific situation?"

The maturity curve

Teams naturally progress through stages of inference stack complexity. Understanding where you are on this curve prevents both premature optimization and unnecessary suffering.

Stage 1, Single instance: One vLLM pod, one model, a Kubernetes Service in front of it. No routing intelligence, no autoscaling, no disaggregation. This handles proof-of-concept and low-traffic internal tools. Operational overhead: minimal. Performance ceiling: one GPU's worth of throughput.

Stage 2, Replicated with smart routing: Multiple vLLM replicas behind an EPP. Cache-aware routing, load balancing, basic health checking. This is where most teams should start for production traffic. Adding the EPP to a replicated deployment typically improves TTFT by 40-70% for multi-turn workloads at nearly zero additional GPU cost. The improvement comes from routing intelligence, not more hardware.

Stage 3, Observable and scalable: Prometheus metrics, Grafana dashboards, HPA autoscaling on inference-specific metrics. This is the minimum viable production stack. You can now see what is happening, react to traffic changes, and diagnose issues without guessing.

Stage 4, Disaggregated and specialized: Separate prefill and decode pools, KV cache transfer, specialized routing. This is for teams with latency targets that cannot be met with a unified pool. The operational complexity roughly doubles, but the latency improvement for long-context workloads can be 3-5x.

⚠ When to stop adding complexity The "golden path" for 80% of production use cases is Stage 3: replicated vLLM, EPP for cache-aware routing, Prometheus monitoring, and HPA autoscaling. This configuration handles most traffic patterns, provides adequate latency for most SLOs, and is operationally tractable for a small team. Only move to Stage 4 if you have specific, measured evidence that your latency targets require it. Premature disaggregation is one of the most common sources of unnecessary operational pain in inference platforms.

Quantifying the tradeoff

Each component has a rough cost-benefit profile:

Component Ops Overhead Performance Gain When It Pays Off
EPP Low 40-70% TTFT improvement Multi-turn, RAG, any prefix reuse
Prometheus + Grafana Low-Medium Enables all future optimization Any production deployment
HPA Autoscaler Medium 30-50% cost reduction Variable traffic patterns
Gateway API Medium Security, rate limiting, traffic management External-facing endpoints
Disaggregation High 3-5x TTFT improvement on long contexts Long context + aggressive TTFT SLO
LoRA routing Medium 5-10x cost reduction vs. separate deployments Multiple fine-tuned models on shared base

6. Migration Paths: How to Evolve Your Stack

The worst way to build an inference platform is to design the final architecture on day one and try to deploy it all at once. The right approach is incremental: start with the simplest architecture that meets your current needs, instrument it, measure it, and add complexity only when measurement shows you need it. Here is the concrete progression.

Phase 1: Single vLLM instance

Trigger: You have a model and you need to serve it. Architecture: One vLLM pod, one Kubernetes Service, one model. What you gain: A working inference endpoint. What you risk: No redundancy, no scaling, no routing intelligence. Good for development, staging, and low-traffic internal tools.

# Phase 1: Minimal vLLM deployment

  name: vllm-server
spec:
  replicas: 1
  template:
    spec:
      containers:
      - name: vllm
        image: vllm/vllm-openai:latest
        args:
        - "--model"
        - "meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct"
        - "--enable-prefix-caching"
        resources:
          limits:
            nvidia.com/gpu: 1
💰 Real talk If you're still on Phase 1 after 6 months, you're leaving money on the table. A single vLLM instance with round-robin routing across replicas wastes 30-60% of your prefill compute on cache misses. Adding the EPP is a one-day job that pays for itself immediately. Don't let "it works fine" be the enemy of "it could work 2x better for zero extra GPU cost."

Phase 2: Add EPP for cache-aware routing

Trigger: You scale to multiple replicas and notice that TTFT varies wildly between requests, or you start serving multi-turn conversations and realize each turn is re-computing the full context. What you gain: The EPP tracks KV cache state across all replicas and routes requests to the pod with the longest matching cached prefix. For multi-turn workloads, this is typically the single biggest performance improvement available, often larger than upgrading GPU hardware. What you risk: The EPP is an additional component that needs to be healthy for routing to work. If it goes down, you fall back to default Kubernetes routing (functional, but not cache-aware).

Phase 3: Add monitoring and alerting

Trigger: You are running in production and you need to answer questions like "why was latency high at 3 PM?" or "are we going to need more GPUs next month?" What you gain: Visibility into TTFT distributions, cache hit rates, queue depths, GPU utilization, and throughput trends. You can set alerts on p95 TTFT, queue depth thresholds, and GPU memory utilization. What you risk: Prometheus and Grafana are mature, well-understood tools, but they need storage, retention policies, and dashboard maintenance. Budget 4-8 hours for initial setup and 1-2 hours per week for ongoing maintenance.

Phase 4: Add autoscaling

Trigger: Your traffic is variable enough that a fixed replica count either wastes GPUs during off-peak hours or drops requests during peaks. What you gain: HPA scales replica count based on vLLM-exported metrics (queue depth, pending requests). You stop paying for idle GPUs during off-peak hours and stop dropping requests during peak. What you risk: Autoscaling with GPUs is slower than autoscaling with CPUs. New vLLM pods need to load model weights, which takes 30 seconds to several minutes depending on model size. Your scaling policy needs to account for this startup time with appropriate stabilization windows and predictive scaling.

Phase 5: Add disaggregation

Trigger: Your measured p95 TTFT exceeds your SLO, and profiling shows that long prefill operations are blocking decode operations on shared GPUs. This typically manifests when serving models above 30B parameters with prompts above 2,000 tokens and TTFT targets below 500ms. What you gain: Prefill and decode run on separate GPU pools. Prefill pods are optimized for compute throughput; decode pods are optimized for memory bandwidth. Neither blocks the other. What you risk: You are now managing two GPU pools, KV cache transfer between them, and cross-pool routing logic. Operational complexity roughly doubles. Only pursue this with specific, measured evidence that unified serving cannot meet your SLOs.

Phase 6: Add multi-tenancy

Trigger: A second team wants to serve their fine-tuned adapter on the same infrastructure, or you need to provide isolated model endpoints for different customers or business units. What you gain: LoRA-aware routing allows multiple fine-tuned adapters to share base model weights on the same GPU. The EPP routes requests to replicas that have the requested adapter loaded, combining cache awareness with adapter awareness. Namespace isolation provides security boundaries between tenants. What you risk: Multi-tenant scheduling adds complexity to routing decisions. Adapter loading has a cold-start penalty (typically 1-5 seconds). You need policies for adapter eviction, priority, and resource quotas.

7. Migration Horror Stories: Lessons Learned the Hard Way

Theory is clean. Production is not. The migration path described above looks smooth on paper, but every team that has walked it has encountered sharp edges. These three scenarios are composites drawn from real-world incidents. They illustrate the kinds of failures that don't show up in architecture diagrams but absolutely show up in your pager alerts.

Horror Story #1: The Silent KV Cache Regression

Scenario: KV cache format incompatibility during vLLM version upgrade

A fintech team was running vLLM v0.4.x on a production chatbot handling 50,000 conversations per day. Their prefix cache hit rate was a healthy 72%, and TTFT on multi-turn conversations averaged 140ms after the first turn. They upgraded to vLLM v0.5.x on a Thursday afternoon to pick up a memory efficiency improvement.

Everything looked fine. The pods started, health checks passed, requests were flowing. But the KV cache format had changed between versions, and the rolling update meant that new pods could not reuse cached prefixes computed by old pods. During the 45-minute rollout window, cache hit rate dropped to 8%. TTFT spiked to 680ms. The Prometheus dashboards showed the regression clearly, but the on-call engineer was watching error rates (which stayed at zero) and request counts (which stayed normal). The latency spike was only noticed when a product manager complained that "the chatbot feels slow today."

The deeper problem: The team had alerts on error rates and throughput but not on cache hit rate or TTFT percentiles. The upgrade was functionally correct; every request got a valid response. But the performance characteristics changed silently because the new binary's KV cache layout was incompatible with the old binary's cached prefixes.

The fix: Three changes prevented recurrence. First, they added alerts on prefix cache hit rate with a threshold at 50% (well above their normal 70-75% baseline). Second, they added alerts on p95 TTFT with a threshold at 300ms. Third, they changed their rollout procedure: new vLLM versions now deploy to a canary replica first, run for 30 minutes while cache metrics stabilize, and only proceed to full rollout if cache hit rate and TTFT remain within baseline ranges. The total engineering time to implement these safeguards was about two days.

Horror Story #2: The Configuration Drift Deploy

Scenario: Staging/production config mismatch causes 4x latency regression on deploy day

An e-commerce platform ran a RAG-powered product search assistant. The staging environment had been performing well for weeks: p95 TTFT of 320ms, throughput of 180 requests per second. On deploy day, they promoted the staging configuration to production. Latency immediately jumped to 1,400ms p95. GPU utilization dropped from 78% to 31%. The team spent four hours debugging before finding the root cause.

The staging environment used gpu_memory_utilization: 0.92, which left enough GPU memory for the KV cache to hold approximately 800 concurrent sequences. The production environment's base configuration (set months earlier by a different team member) had gpu_memory_utilization: 0.70, which cut the KV cache capacity nearly in half. With fewer cache slots available, the EPP couldn't maintain session affinity because cached prefixes were being evicted before the next conversation turn arrived. Cache hit rate dropped from 65% to 12%. Every request was doing a full prefill from scratch.

Making things worse, the staging environment used max_model_len: 8192 while production used the default (model's full context length, 32768). This meant production reserved 4x more memory per sequence slot for potential long contexts that never actually arrived, further reducing the number of concurrent sequences the KV cache could support.

The fix: The team implemented three changes. First, they version-controlled all vLLM configuration parameters in a shared Helm values file with environment-specific overrides, eliminating the possibility of invisible divergence. Second, they added a pre-deploy validation step that diffs staging and production configs and flags any parameter mismatches for explicit review. Third, they added a Grafana dashboard panel specifically for "KV cache capacity versus utilization" that makes memory allocation issues immediately visible. The entire investigation and fix took about a week of engineering time, most of which was spent understanding the subtle interaction between gpu_memory_utilization, max_model_len, and effective cache capacity.

Horror Story #3: The LoRA Adapter Mismatch

Scenario: LoRA adapter weight format mismatch causes silent output degradation

A healthcare company served four LoRA adapters on a shared Llama 3.1 70B base model: one for clinical note summarization, one for patient intake triage, one for medical coding (ICD-10), and one for insurance prior authorization. The medical coding adapter was retrained on a new dataset and the updated weights were pushed to the model registry. Within an hour, the medical coding outputs went from 94% accuracy on their test suite to 23%. But the model wasn't erroring. It was producing valid ICD-10 codes. They were just the wrong codes.

The root cause was a mismatch between the LoRA adapter's target modules and the base model's expected layer names. The retraining pipeline used an updated version of the training framework that named attention layers differently (q_proj and v_proj versus the expected self_attn.q_proj and self_attn.v_proj). vLLM loaded the adapter without errors, but the adapter weights were being applied to the wrong layers, effectively producing a model that was neither the base model nor the fine-tuned model but something in between. The outputs were syntactically valid (they looked like ICD-10 codes) but semantically wrong.

The failure was caught only because a medical coder on the team happened to review a batch of outputs and noticed that knee replacement surgeries were being coded as dental procedures. There was no automated quality check on adapter outputs, no A/B comparison between adapter versions, and no validation that adapter weights were being applied to the expected layers.

The fix: The team implemented a comprehensive adapter lifecycle process. First, every adapter now includes a test suite of 50 known-good input/output pairs that must pass before the adapter is promoted to production. Second, they added a pre-load validation step in their deployment pipeline that verifies adapter target modules match the base model's layer names exactly. Third, new adapter versions deploy to a shadow replica first, where outputs are compared against the previous version. If the output divergence exceeds a configured threshold (measured by embedding similarity), the deployment is halted automatically. Fourth, they pinned their training framework version and added CI checks that flag any changes to layer naming conventions. The total investment was about two weeks of engineering time, but it prevented a recurrence of what could have been a patient safety issue.

⚠ Common thread All three horror stories share the same pattern: the system continued to function (no errors, no crashes) while producing degraded results. Traditional monitoring (error rates, uptime, throughput) caught none of them. The lesson is clear. Inference-specific observability is not optional. You need to monitor cache hit rates, TTFT percentiles, output quality metrics, and configuration consistency. The failure mode of inference systems is rarely "it stops working." It is almost always "it keeps working, just worse."

8. Deep Comparison with Alternative Stacks

Let me be blunt here. If you are running on Kubernetes, llm-d is the answer. Full stop. No other stack gives you cache-aware cross-replica routing, LoRA multi-tenancy, and prefill/decode disaggregation as composable, Kubernetes-native primitives. But you deserve to understand why, so let's walk through the alternatives honestly and in depth.

TensorRT-LLM + Triton Inference Server

NVIDIA's native stack delivers the highest single-GPU performance on NVIDIA hardware. TensorRT-LLM compiles models into optimized CUDA kernels with aggressive operator fusion, and Triton provides a mature model-serving framework with dynamic batching, model versioning, and ensemble pipelines.

Where it wins: Raw inference speed on NVIDIA GPUs, especially for models that benefit from FP8 quantization and custom CUDA kernels. The performance gap is real, typically 10-25% higher throughput per GPU compared to vLLM for supported models. Triton also has a mature model management story with versioning, A/B testing, and ensemble pipelines that chain multiple models together. For organizations deeply invested in the NVIDIA ecosystem with dedicated MLOps teams, this stack offers the tightest possible integration with NVIDIA hardware capabilities.

Where it falls short: TensorRT-LLM requires model compilation, which adds minutes to hours to the deployment pipeline and makes rapid model iteration difficult. The compilation step also means you cannot hot-swap models or adapters without recompilation; every LoRA adapter change requires a new compilation cycle. Triton's routing is not KV-cache-aware. It does dynamic batching within a single instance but does not understand cross-replica cache state. The stack is NVIDIA-only, which is a meaningful limitation as AMD GPUs (MI300X in particular) become more available and cost-competitive in cloud environments. Multi-tenant LoRA serving requires manual configuration rather than dynamic routing. If you want the TRT-LLM speed advantage with intelligent routing, you're building significant custom infrastructure yourself.

Community and ecosystem: NVIDIA has significant resources behind TensorRT-LLM, but the project operates under NVIDIA's proprietary development model. Community contributions are limited compared to open-source alternatives. Issue resolution depends on NVIDIA's internal prioritization. The Triton ecosystem is mature, with strong documentation and examples, but it is designed around NVIDIA's hardware assumptions.

Production readiness: High for single-instance deployments on NVIDIA hardware. Medium for multi-replica orchestrated deployments, where you need to bring your own routing, scaling, and cache management. The Triton Kubernetes operator exists but provides basic orchestration rather than inference-aware routing.

💡 When to choose TRT-LLM over llm-d If you are (a) running exclusively on NVIDIA hardware, (b) serving a single model without LoRA adapters, (c) optimizing for maximum single-GPU throughput above all else, and (d) willing to accept longer deployment cycles for the compilation step, TRT-LLM can deliver 10-25% better raw throughput. But the moment you need multi-replica routing, LoRA multi-tenancy, or multi-vendor GPU support, the advantage evaporates.

SGLang

SGLang is a strong inference engine with excellent prefix caching (RadixAttention) and structured generation support. It particularly shines for workloads that involve constrained decoding: JSON output, grammar-guided generation, and fill-in-the-middle tasks.

Where it wins: Structured output performance, prefix caching efficiency within a single instance, and developer ergonomics for prompt programming patterns. SGLang's RadixAttention provides arguably the best single-instance prefix caching implementation available, organizing cached prefixes in a radix tree that enables efficient longest-prefix matching. For workloads that are primarily single-instance and involve heavy structured generation (JSON schema enforcement, regex-constrained output, grammar-guided decoding), SGLang can outperform vLLM meaningfully. The programming model is also elegant, with first-class support for prompt composition, branching, and constrained generation.

Where it falls short: SGLang is primarily a single-process inference engine. It does not provide distributed orchestration, cross-replica cache-aware routing, Kubernetes-native deployment primitives, or autoscaling. For production deployments with multiple replicas, you need to build the routing and scaling layer yourself or integrate with an external orchestrator. There is no equivalent of the EPP for cross-replica cache coordination. When you scale to multiple SGLang instances, each instance maintains its own independent radix tree, and there is no mechanism to route a request to the instance that holds the relevant cached prefix. You lose the primary advantage of RadixAttention the moment you need more than one replica. Great engine, no platform.

Community and ecosystem: SGLang has a vibrant research community, with regular contributions from academic groups and active development. However, the community is smaller than vLLM's, and production deployment tooling (Helm charts, Kubernetes operators, monitoring integrations) is less mature. Most SGLang deployments in production use custom deployment scripts rather than standardized infrastructure-as-code.

Production readiness: High for single-instance workloads, especially those involving structured generation. Low-medium for multi-replica production deployments, where the absence of distributed orchestration, routing intelligence, and operational tooling means significant custom engineering is required.

Ray Serve + vLLM

Ray Serve wraps vLLM in Ray's distributed computing framework, adding autoscaling, model multiplexing, and integration with the broader Ray ML ecosystem (Ray Train, Ray Data). This is a natural fit for teams already invested in Ray for training and data processing.

Where it wins: Integration with Ray's ML pipeline tools, model composition (chaining models, pre/post-processing), and autoscaling across heterogeneous resources. If your organization already uses Ray for distributed training, data processing, or hyperparameter tuning, Ray Serve provides a unified control plane that spans the entire ML lifecycle from training to serving. Ray's model multiplexing allows serving multiple models from a single deployment, and its resource management can handle heterogeneous GPU types within a single cluster. The autoscaling story is also mature, with support for scaling based on custom metrics and sophisticated placement strategies.

Where it falls short: Ray adds significant memory and CPU overhead for its distributed runtime. A Ray cluster requires head nodes, worker nodes, the Global Control Store (GCS), and the Ray dashboard, all consuming resources that could otherwise serve inference requests. More importantly, Ray Serve's routing is not inference-aware. It does not understand KV cache state, prefix overlap, or LoRA adapter locality. Routing decisions are based on generic load metrics (queue depth, replica count), not the inference-specific signals that determine actual latency. For teams not already using Ray, the learning curve and operational overhead are substantial. You're paying for a general-purpose distributed computing framework when what you need is an inference-aware routing layer. Ray's failure modes are also complex; a GCS failure can cascade across the entire cluster in ways that are difficult to diagnose without deep Ray expertise.

Community and ecosystem: Ray has a large, active community backed by Anyscale. Documentation is extensive, and there are many examples of production Ray deployments. However, the intersection of "Ray Serve" and "LLM inference at scale" is still relatively niche. Most production Ray Serve + vLLM deployments are at organizations that were already Ray shops, not organizations that chose Ray specifically for inference serving.

Production readiness: High if your team already has Ray expertise and infrastructure. Medium otherwise. The learning curve for Ray operations (debugging GCS issues, understanding placement groups, tuning autoscaling policies) is steep, and these skills are orthogonal to inference serving expertise. KubeRay provides Kubernetes integration, but it adds another layer of operational complexity on top of Ray's own complexity.

Triton Inference Server (standalone)

Triton can also be deployed as a standalone model server without TensorRT-LLM, serving models in ONNX, TensorFlow SavedModel, or PyTorch formats. In this configuration, it functions as a general-purpose model server with dynamic batching, model versioning, and ensemble support.

Where it wins: Multi-framework model serving. If you need to serve a mix of LLMs, vision models, embedding models, and traditional ML models from a single infrastructure, Triton's multi-backend support is genuinely useful. It can serve an ONNX embedding model, a PyTorch classification model, and a vLLM-based LLM from the same deployment, chained together in an ensemble pipeline. The model repository pattern (versioned model artifacts in shared storage) provides clean model lifecycle management. Health checking, readiness probing, and model loading/unloading are well-implemented.

Where it falls short: When used specifically for LLM inference, standalone Triton offers no advantages over vLLM and several disadvantages. It does not implement PagedAttention, continuous batching for autoregressive generation, or prefix caching. Its dynamic batching is designed for fixed-shape tensor inputs, not variable-length token sequences. For LLM workloads, Triton is essentially a container that runs vLLM or TRT-LLM as a backend, adding a layer of indirection without adding inference-specific routing intelligence. The Kubernetes operator provides basic pod management but not cache-aware routing or LoRA-aware scheduling.

Community and ecosystem: Triton has a large user base in the traditional ML serving space (recommendation systems, vision models, tabular ML). The LLM-specific community is smaller and largely overlaps with the TRT-LLM community. Documentation is thorough but assumes familiarity with NVIDIA's model repository structure and configuration format.

Production readiness: High for mixed-model serving workloads. For LLM-specific workloads, it adds operational overhead without adding inference-specific value. If your primary workload is LLM serving and you don't need multi-framework model support, deploying Triton as a wrapper around vLLM adds complexity without proportional benefit.

Ollama

Ollama is the right tool for local development, experimentation, and single-user desktop inference. It provides a beautiful developer experience: one command to download and serve a model, a simple API, and broad model support.

Where it wins: Developer experience, local development workflow, ease of setup.

Where it falls short: Ollama is not designed for production serving. It lacks multi-replica support, cache-aware routing, autoscaling, GPU sharing, LoRA adapter routing, and the monitoring and observability primitives that production workloads require. Using Ollama in production is like using SQLite as your production database; it works until it does not, and the failure mode is sudden. Don't do it.

llm-d

llm-d's differentiator is that it treats inference serving as a distributed systems problem, not just a single-GPU optimization problem. The EPP's cache-aware routing, the Gateway API integration, and the Kubernetes-native deployment model address the gap between "inference engine" and "inference platform."

Where it wins: Cache-aware cross-replica routing, LoRA-aware multi-tenant serving, Kubernetes-native deployment and scaling, composable architecture that lets you adopt components incrementally, and multi-vendor GPU support (NVIDIA and AMD).

Where it has limits: llm-d requires Kubernetes. If your team does not have Kubernetes expertise, the learning curve is real. The raw single-GPU inference speed is vLLM's, which is excellent but not the absolute fastest on NVIDIA hardware (TensorRT-LLM edges it out on some workloads). The project is relatively new (CNCF Sandbox stage), so the ecosystem of third-party integrations is still growing. But it is growing fast, and the CNCF backing means it is not going anywhere.

The total cost of ownership includes GPU compute costs, engineering time for setup and maintenance, operational overhead for day-to-day running, and any licensing or vendor lock-in considerations. 10. Decision Checklist for Enterprises

Before committing to an inference architecture, work through these ten questions. Each one maps to a specific architectural decision. If you cannot answer a question, that is itself a finding: it means you need to gather more information before you can make an informed decision.

  • 1 What model(s) will you serve? Determines GPU memory requirements, tensor parallelism degree, and minimum deployment cost. A 7B model on one GPU is a fundamentally different operational problem than a 70B model across four.
  • 2 What are your latency SLOs? Defines whether you need unified serving (TTFT > 1s), cache-aware routing (TTFT 200ms-1s), or disaggregation (TTFT < 200ms on long contexts). Be specific: p50, p95, p99.
  • 3 How many teams or tenants will share the infrastructure? Single-tenant is simple. Multi-tenant requires LoRA-aware routing, namespace isolation, resource quotas, and chargeback mechanisms. Each tenant added increases routing complexity.
  • 4 What is your GPU budget? Determines replica count, redundancy level, and whether you can afford dedicated prefill/decode pools. Also determines how aggressively you should pursue efficiency optimizations like quantization and cache-aware routing.
  • 5 Cloud or on-premise? Cloud offers elastic GPU provisioning but at higher per-hour cost. On-premise offers lower per-hour cost but requires capacity planning and hardware procurement lead times. Hybrid adds networking complexity.
  • 6 What is your ops team's Kubernetes maturity? llm-d is Kubernetes-native, which is a strength if your team lives in Kubernetes and a barrier if they do not. Be honest about this. The best architecture is one your team can operate at 2 AM.
  • 7 Do you need LoRA adapters? If multiple fine-tuned models share a base model, LoRA adapters with dynamic routing can reduce GPU cost by 5-10x compared to serving separate model instances. This determines whether you need LoRA-aware routing in the EPP.
  • 8 What is your availability target? 99.9% availability requires at least 2 replicas with health checking and failover. 99.99% requires geographic redundancy or multi-zone deployment. Each nine adds operational and cost overhead.
  • 9 Do you have existing monitoring infrastructure? If you already run Prometheus and Grafana, integrating vLLM metrics is straightforward. If you are starting from scratch, budget 1-2 weeks for monitoring setup, dashboard design, and alert tuning.
  • 10 What is your scaling profile? Steady traffic can use fixed replicas. Predictable peaks (business hours, campaign launches) can use scheduled scaling. Unpredictable spikes require reactive HPA with appropriate stabilization windows and startup time buffers.

11. Conclusion

Here's what I need you to take away from this post: there is no universally correct inference stack. But there is a very short list of practical architectures that cover the vast majority of production use cases. Your model size and your latency target get you 80% of the way to the right answer. The other three dimensions (traffic shape, GPU budget, and ops maturity) refine the details.

If you're on Kubernetes, llm-d gives you the components to build the right architecture from Kubernetes-native primitives. Start with vLLM and the EPP. Add Prometheus. Add autoscaling. That's Stage 3, and it handles most production workloads. Only add disaggregation when your measurements demand it. Each step should be triggered by evidence, not aspiration.
